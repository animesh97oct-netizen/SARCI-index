

SARCI — South & Central Asia Risk Composite Index
A White Paper by Animesh Banerjee | 2025–26 Research Project

Why I Built This
Most risk indices have a dirty secret: they don't actually measure risk. They measure how much newspapers talk about risk. The Caldara-Iacoviello GPR — one of the most cited geopolitical risk indices in academic finance — works by counting articles in eight newspapers that mention words like "war," "terrorism," or "nuclear threat." That's not a risk measurement. That's a media sentiment tracker dressed up in academic language.
The problem goes deeper than methodology. Global indices like BlackRock's GRI or the S&P Geopolitical Risk Score rank 190+ countries in a single list, which means Afghanistan and Bhutan sit in the same analytical bucket as Venezuela and Mali. The sub-regional dynamics — the BRI corridor tension, the post-US-withdrawal spillover from Afghanistan, the garment supply chain concentration in South Asia — simply vanish inside the aggregation.
So I built something different. SARCI is a 13-country, four-pillar composite index built entirely from raw institutional data — IMF, ACLED, World Bank, UN Comtrade — with every scoring threshold documented and every number traceable to a primary source. It doesn't measure what people say about risk. It measures where risk actually lives.

The 13 Countries — and Why These Specifically
The country list isn't arbitrary. These 13 countries share three overlapping strategic geographies that no existing index treats as a single unit of analysis: the Belt and Road Initiative corridor, the OTEXA textile supply chain (Bangladesh, India, Pakistan together supply roughly 20% of US apparel imports), and the post-2021 Afghanistan risk zone that radiates instability into both South and Central Asia.
Myanmar was added mid-project after ACLED data showed 20,457 conflict deaths in 2024 — making it the #2 most conflict-affected country on Earth. Leaving it out would have been an analytical error, not a principled exclusion.
The 13 countries range from Bhutan (zero ACLED events, top-5 globally on political stability) to Afghanistan (economy so collapsed that the IMF suppresses its data entirely). That spread is the point. Any meaningful risk framework needs to capture both extremes and everything in between.

The Four Pillars — What They Measure and Why
Pillar A — Economic Stress looks at three IMF numbers: GDP growth, inflation, and fiscal balance as a share of GDP. Together, they tell you whether a country has the economic breathing room to absorb a shock — or whether it's already on the edge. Sri Lanka's score of 5.00 on this pillar isn't a guess; it reflects an economy that literally defaulted in 2022 and is still under IMF intensive care.
Pillar B — Supply Chain Exposure is the pillar most indices ignore entirely. It asks three questions: How dependent is this country on a single export? How much of its imports come from China? And how much of the US apparel market does it supply? These questions matter enormously for investors, procurement managers, and trade policy analysts — people who need to know if a disruption in Dhaka or Karachi immediately ripples into American retail stores.
Pillar C — Security Environment uses ACLED data — the gold standard for political violence research — but critically, it normalises everything per 100,000 people. This is the same approach WHO uses for disease burden, the same approach SIPRI uses for armed conflict deaths. Without normalisation, India with 1.4 billion people looks more dangerous than Myanmar on raw counts alone. That would be analytically absurd.
Pillar D — Political Stability uses the World Bank's WGI Political Stability indicator, which pulls from 30+ institutional sources including the EIU, Freedom House, and Gallup. It measures one thing clearly: what is the probability that a government will be destabilised or overthrown by violent or unconstitutional means?

How the Scoring Works
Every sub-indicator gets a score from 1 to 5 — VERY LOW to CRITICAL. The thresholds aren't invented; they're anchored to real institutional benchmarks. The 3% fiscal deficit threshold comes from IMF programme conditionality. The conflict fatality thresholds mirror WHO epidemiological rate classifications. The ACLED tiers are ACLED's own official system.
Each pillar averages its three sub-indicators. Then the four pillar scores are averaged with equal 25% weights to produce the final SARCI score. Equal weighting was a deliberate choice, not a lazy default. Economic crises cause conflict; conflict causes economic collapse; political instability disrupts supply chains. These pillars are endogenous to each other, which means there's no defensible causal hierarchy to weight against. Equal weighting is the most transparent approach — it's also the same approach the UN Human Development Index uses.

Why Every Decision Here Has a Reason
Every decision in this index — the 13-country scope, the four pillars, the per-100k normalisation, the equal weights, the specific scoring thresholds — was made for a documented reason, not convenience. That's what separates an analytical tool from a ranking exercise.
The BRI corridor framing explains the country scope. The endogeneity of economic and security risk explains the equal weights. The need to compare India and Myanmar fairly explains the per-100k normalisation. The IMF's own programme conditionality explains the 3% fiscal deficit threshold. When every methodological choice has a reason, the index can be challenged, improved, and trusted — which is the only standard that matters in serious policy and investment analysis.

What the Results Actually Say
Myanmar and Afghanistan Are Alone at the Top
Myanmar maxes out at 5.00 on both Security and Political Stability simultaneously — the only country in the dataset to do this across two pillars at once. A civil war producing over 20,000 deaths in a single year, combined with a military junta that sits at the 4.7th percentile globally on political stability, creates a risk profile where every channel of vulnerability is firing at the same time. Afghanistan's score of 5.00 on Economic Stress reflects something even starker: the IMF has suppressed its economic data because the Taliban government doesn't report it. When the data collection apparatus itself has collapsed, you're beyond conventional risk frameworks.
Bangladesh Is the Most Surprising Country in the Dataset
Bangladesh ranks #3 — ahead of Pakistan, ahead of India — and most analysts would not predict that. The reason is exactly what composite frameworks are designed to reveal: Bangladesh's risk isn't concentrated in one place, it's spread across three pillars simultaneously. It supplies 10.53% of all US apparel imports, the highest in the dataset, making it directly exposed to any shift in US trade policy. It ranks #18 globally on ACLED's conflict index. And Sheikh Hasina's ouster in August 2024 triggered a political crisis that pushed Bangladesh's WGI score down sharply. No single-dimension index would surface this. The composite framework does.
Sri Lanka Scores High on Economic Stress But Medium Overall — That's Actually the Point
Sri Lanka scores 5.00 on Economic Stress, but its Security score is 1.00 and Political Stability is 2.00, producing a composite of just 2.58 — MEDIUM. Some people would call this a flaw in the methodology. It isn't. It correctly captures that Sri Lanka's 2022 default crisis is not currently translating into active armed conflict or political violence. It's a country in an IMF recovery programme that is, for now, stabilising. The index flags it as "stable-for-now," not "resolved" — and that distinction matters enormously for investment and policy decisions.
Central Asia Is Systematically Overestimated as a Risk Zone
Western risk models routinely treat Central Asia as a single high-risk bloc, largely because of geographic proximity to Afghanistan. The SARCI data disagrees. Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Kyrgyzstan all score LOW. Their security environments are near-zero on ACLED. Uzbekistan's fiscal balance is –0.8% of GDP — healthier than most European countries. Bhutan, which Western analysts rarely even include in risk frameworks, scores VERY LOW and ranks among the top five countries globally on political stability.
India's MEDIUM Score Is Analytically Defensible
India scoring 2.50 — MEDIUM — will surprise people who associate South Asia with high risk. The methodology separates scale from intensity deliberately. India's 3,608 ACLED events translate to just 0.25 events per 100,000 people given a 1.4 billion population. The real vulnerability in India's score is its fiscal deficit of –7.4% of GDP, which drags its Economic Stress score upward. The composite correctly identifies India as a large, resilient economy with structural fiscal pressure — not a crisis state.

What This Index Cannot Do (Yet)
SARCI is a snapshot. It tells you where risk sits right now — it doesn't tell you whether a country is getting better or worse. That's the most important limitation. A country with a score of 3.00 that has been falling for three years is a completely different risk picture from a country with the same score that has been rising. Future versions should include year-on-year delta scores to capture trajectory.
The equal weighting is transparent but it's also a simplification. An investor pricing political risk into a bond spread might rationally weight Economic Stress at 40%. A humanitarian organisation planning crisis response might weight Security at 50%. A sensitivity analysis — showing how the rankings shift under five or six alternative weighting schemes — would make this index significantly more robust and publishable.
The sub-national problem is real and unsolved. India's national score of 2.50 conceals the fact that Manipur and Jammu & Kashmir would score 5.00 on Security while Gujarat and Tamil Nadu would score 1.00. National-level indices lose this granularity entirely. That's a structural limitation of any country-level framework — not unique to SARCI, but worth naming honestly.
